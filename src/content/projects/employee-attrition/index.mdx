# Introduction

Employee attrition is one of the most relevant topics in human resources analytics because employee turnover has a direct impact on organizational costs, productivity, and knowledge retention. Replacing experienced employees requires time, financial resources, and additional training, making it essential for organizations to understand the factors associated with employees leaving the company.

This case study documents an exploratory data analysis project based on the IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition dataset. Rather than building a predictive machine learning model, the objective was to understand the dataset, identify meaningful patterns, and communicate insights that could support HR decision-making.

Throughout the project, the complete data analysis process is presented, including business understanding, data exploration, data preparation, exploratory analysis, and the final conclusions derived from the findings.

The goal of this case study is not only to present the final visualizations but also to document the reasoning behind each analytical decision.

The analysis was performed using Python and common data analysis libraries, while SQL concepts were incorporated as part of the data exploration process.

# Business Understanding

Employee turnover represents a significant challenge for organizations of all sizes. High attrition rates often translate into increased recruitment costs, reduced team productivity, and the loss of organizational knowledge.

Understanding why employees leave requires looking beyond a single variable. Factors such as job satisfaction, overtime, work-life balance, compensation, career progression, and departmental differences may all contribute to employee decisions.

The primary objective of this project was to explore these relationships through descriptive analytics rather than predictive modeling.

More specifically, the project aimed to answer questions such as:

- Which employee groups experience the highest attrition?
- Does overtime influence turnover?
- Is job satisfaction related to employee retention?
- Which departments are more affected?
- Are demographic variables associated with attrition?

By answering these questions, the project demonstrates how data analysis can support evidence-based HR decisions.

# Data Understanding

## Dataset Overview

The project uses the IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition dataset, a widely used dataset for educational purposes.

The dataset contains demographic information, employment characteristics, performance indicators, and employee satisfaction metrics.

Examples of available variables include:

- Age
- Department
- Business Travel
- Education
- Environment Satisfaction
- Job Satisfaction
- Monthly Income
- Overtime
- Years at Company
- Years Since Last Promotion
- Attrition

The target variable, Attrition, indicates whether an employee left the company.

Although the dataset is relatively clean, understanding each variable before analysis is essential.

Several variables represent categorical information, while others are numerical measurements describing employee characteristics and career progression.

## Data Quality

The first step consisted of evaluating the overall quality of the dataset.

Several aspects were reviewed:

- Missing values
- Duplicate records
- Data types
- Numerical distributions
- Category consistency

Fortunately, the IBM dataset contains very few missing values, making preprocessing relatively straightforward.

Even when data appears clean, validating assumptions remains an important step in every analytical workflow.

Variable distributions were also inspected to identify skewed numerical features and highly imbalanced categorical variables.

# Data Preparation

Although extensive cleaning was not required, several preprocessing tasks were completed before starting the analysis.

These included:

- Reviewing categorical variables.
- Verifying numerical ranges.
- Standardizing data types.
- Creating derived metrics where appropriate.
- Organizing variables for visualization.

Preparing the dataset before analysis improves reproducibility and reduces the likelihood of introducing inconsistencies later in the project.

Additional exploratory transformations were performed during visualization when grouping employees by department, age range, and years at the company.

# Exploratory Data Analysis

The exploratory analysis focused on identifying relationships between employee characteristics and attrition.

Several visualizations were created to compare employees who left the company against those who remained.

The analysis included:

- Attrition distribution
- Department comparisons
- Monthly income analysis
- Overtime analysis
- Job satisfaction distribution
- Work-life balance comparison
- Age distribution
- Years at company

Each visualization was interpreted individually before combining findings into broader conclusions.

Special attention was given to variables commonly referenced in HR literature, such as overtime and job satisfaction.

While some relationships appeared visually strong, others suggested only weak associations, reinforcing the importance of interpreting descriptive statistics carefully.

# Key Findings

Several recurring patterns emerged throughout the analysis.

Employees working overtime appeared more likely to leave the organization.

Departments exhibited different attrition rates, suggesting that organizational context may influence employee retention.

Employees with lower job satisfaction generally showed higher turnover proportions.

Years at the company also appeared related to attrition, particularly among relatively new employees.

It is important to emphasize that these observations describe associations rather than causal relationships.

The project intentionally focuses on descriptive analytics and avoids drawing unsupported causal conclusions.

# Conclusions

This case study demonstrates how exploratory data analysis can generate meaningful business insights without relying on complex predictive models.

Even relatively clean datasets require structured exploration to understand relationships between variables and identify potential business concerns.

The findings highlight several factors that HR professionals may investigate further, including overtime practices, employee satisfaction, departmental differences, and career progression.

Beyond the specific conclusions, this project also serves as an example of a structured analytics workflow, documenting each stage of the process from business understanding to final interpretation.

Future work could extend this analysis by incorporating predictive models, feature engineering techniques, or additional HR datasets to validate the observed patterns.
